# Competitive Analysis Framework

Competitive analysis is the discipline of evaluating direct competitors, indirect competitors, and substitutes across multiple dimensions so you can find a better market position.

## Why It Matters

You do not compete only with similar products. You also compete with alternatives for the user's time, money, and attention.

## When to Use It

- When launching a new product
- When users are churning
- When planning the yearly roadmap
- Before fundraising or a strategic review
- When entering a new market
- Before changing pricing

## What to Compare

| Dimension | Key Question | Typical Sources |
|---|---|---|
| Target Users and Positioning | Who do they serve and what job do they solve? | Website, app store reviews, ads |
| Core Feature Set | What is truly essential and what is noise? | Product trial, help docs, reports |
| User Journey | Where does the experience break down? | Interviews, support feedback, reviews |
| Business Model | How do they make money? | Pricing pages, financial reports, industry reports |
| Market Size and Growth | How big and fast is the market? | SimilarWeb, app data, filings |
| Technical Barriers | What keeps others from copying them? | Technical blogs, patents, job posts |

## How to Run a Competitive Analysis

1. Define the scope clearly.
2. Build a comparison matrix.
3. Use the product yourself.
4. Add market and financial data.
5. Extract differentiation opportunities and risks.

## Notion Case Study

Notion succeeded by combining ideas that were often split across several tools:

- Confluence-style team knowledge management
- Evernote-style personal note taking
- Docs, spreadsheets, and task views in one place
- Flexible blocks, templates, and multi-device sync

Its real advantage was not one isolated feature. It was the combination of workflow flexibility and strong product design.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| Looking only at surface features | UX quality and workflow matter too |
| Ignoring new entrants | Future competitors are often the real threat |
| Copying feature lists | The logic behind the product matters more than the list |
| Doing it once and never updating | The market changes too fast |
| Ignoring data | Opinions are not enough |
| Over-optimism | Star products in one category do not guarantee success in another |
