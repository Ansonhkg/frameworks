# DACI Decision Framework

DACI defines who drives a decision, who approves it, who contributes input, and who needs to know the result. It exists to make decisions faster and clearer.

## Why Decisions Stall

Meetings often end with "let's discuss this again" because no one knows who owns the decision.

DACI solves that by assigning one clear role to each person involved.

## The Four Roles

| Role | Responsibility | Count |
|---|---|---|
| Driver | Pushes the decision forward, gathers input, and coordinates the process | 1 |
| Approver | Makes the final call | 1, at most 2 |
| Contributor | Provides expertise, analysis, and input | As needed |
| Informed | Receives the decision after it is made | As needed |

## When to Use It

Use DACI when:

- The decision needs input from several teams
- There is disagreement or risk of deadlock
- The decision affects more than one group
- You need a clear owner and a clear approver

## Intuit Example

Intuit used DACI to speed up major acquisition and product decisions.

Before DACI, decisions could take months. After DACI, the process became much shorter because everyone knew who was driving, who was approving, and who was simply contributing.

## Six Ways DACI Fails

1. Do not assign more than two approvers.
2. The driver is not the approver.
3. Too many contributors slow the process down.
4. Informed people should not interfere after the decision is made.
5. Define roles before the meeting starts.
6. Once the approver decides, do not reopen the decision unless the scope changes.
