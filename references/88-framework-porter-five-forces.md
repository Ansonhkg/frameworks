# Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces is a framework for evaluating how attractive an industry is and how much profit it can support. It looks at five sources of competitive pressure.

## The Five Forces

| Force | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| Industry Rivalry | How intense the competition is among existing players |
| Threat of New Entrants | How easy it is for new companies to enter |
| Threat of Substitutes | How easily customers can switch to another solution |
| Bargaining Power of Suppliers | How much leverage suppliers have |
| Bargaining Power of Buyers | How much leverage customers have |

## Why It Matters

Some industries stay profitable for years, while others remain under pressure no matter how hard companies work. The Five Forces framework explains why.

## When to Use It

- Before entering a new market
- When shaping competitive strategy
- During investment analysis
- In M&A due diligence
- When planning product positioning and pricing

## iPhone and the Smartphone Market

Apple's iPhone changed the smartphone industry by reshaping all five forces.

| Force | Situation |
|---|---|
| Rivalry | Strong competition from Nokia, BlackBerry, Motorola, and others |
| New Entrants | High entry barriers, but Android lowered some technical barriers |
| Substitutes | Low at the time because smartphones offered a new level of capability |
| Suppliers | Apple depended on key component suppliers, but its scale gave it leverage |
| Buyers | Low buyer power because the ecosystem increased switching costs |

## How to Run the Analysis

1. Define the industry boundary.
2. Score each of the five forces.
3. Draw the force map or pentagon.
4. Turn the analysis into strategy choices.
5. Revisit the analysis regularly because the market changes.

## History

| Period | Development |
|---|---|
| 1979 | Michael Porter publishes *Competitive Strategy* and introduces the framework |
| 1980s | Consulting firms adopt it as a standard tool |
| 1990s | It becomes a staple in MBA and strategy courses |
| 2000s to present | The framework adapts to software, e-commerce, and platform markets |
