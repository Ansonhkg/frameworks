# POV Statement

Use the three-part formula "user + need + insight" to define the core problem precisely and keep the team aligned on what needs to be solved.

**Author**: Antoine Dantan  
**Tags**: research tools, needs definition, design methods, team collaboration  
**Views**: 7.8K

## Part 1

### The team has done the research, but still disagrees on the problem

After user research, teams often know the data but still disagree on the core problem. One person thinks onboarding is the issue, another thinks search should be rebuilt, and another thinks the value proposition should be redefined.

This is not just a communication problem. Research is often exploratory: it exposes real issues, but it does not automatically produce a final answer. The gap between raw data and the problem statement is real.

POV Statement is a filter and translator for that gap. It defines who the user is, what they need, and what insight explains why the problem matters.

## Part 2

### The three-part formula

POV statements are made of three parts: a target user, the user's need, and the insight that explains it.

#### Diagram

User profile
- Target user + background + context
- Who are they? What situation are they in?
- Example: "For urban professionals who commute more than one hour every day..."

Core need
- What do they truly want?
- Example: "...they need a way to..."

Unexpected insight
- Why does this matter?
- Example: "...because they feel..."

## Part 3

### When should you use it?

POV is the bridge from user research to actionable design questions. Use it during the Define stage, after Empathize.

Research -> POV -> Brainstorming

## Part 4

### IDEO x Shimano

In 2005, Shimano asked IDEO to research innovation for its high-end bike brand Claris. Shimano wanted to understand the real needs of urban riders and explore how technology could improve the experience.

IDEO used the Double Diamond style of research: observe, interview, and immerse deeply in the daily habits, pain points, and needs of urban cyclists. The research showed that many city riders were not racing athletes. They cared more about convenience, safety, and social identity than raw speed.

From that, IDEO wrote a strong POV:

"For urban professionals who commute more than one hour every day, they need a bicycle solution that lets them move easily through a busy city while keeping an elegant image, because they want efficient travel without giving up the joy and freedom of riding."

That POV defined the user group, the core need, and the deeper motivation. Shimano and IDEO then developed a range of innovations such as foldable e-bikes, smart navigation, and modular accessories.

## Part 5

### Five common mistakes

**1. Too broad or vague**
- Bad: "Provide a better social experience for young people"
- Good: A clear POV for urban professionals with a specific commute and need

**2. No user perspective**
- Bad: "We need a more efficient search feature"
- Good: A POV for researchers who must find specific content quickly inside a large information set

**3. Ignoring emotional factors**
- Bad: "Provide better healthcare services for older adults"
- Good: A POV for people who are worried about their health but afraid of going to the hospital

**4. No clear scenario**
- Bad: "Provide better learning tools for students"
- Good: A POV for university students who need to complete a large amount of work in limited time

**5. Ignoring the real need**
- Bad: "Provide more convenient booking for travelers"
- Good: A POV for young travelers who want personalized trip planning but do not want too much risk

## Part 6

### From empathy to insight: how Stanford d.school turns "who" into the starting point

POV Statement is a core concept in Stanford d.school's design thinking framework. It is more than a persona or a simple problem definition. It is a bridge from empathy research to concrete design direction.

Traditional business analysis usually starts with market opportunity or technical feasibility. d.school's POV method starts with real user experience and emotion and uses three elements to build a problem frame with depth and scope:

1. User (Who): the target user group, including context, behavior, and emotional state
2. Need: the real pain or desire, not just a surface feature request
3. Insight: the unexpected observation that reveals the deeper need
