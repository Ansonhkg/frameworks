# Crazy 8s

Crazy 8s is a fast ideation exercise. You divide a sheet into eight boxes, set a tight timer, and sketch one distinct idea in each box. The goal is not polish. The goal is to produce a broad set of useful options before your first idea becomes a cage.

## Why Overthinking Kills Creativity

Traditional brainstorming often slows down because people keep evaluating ideas while they are still being generated. Crazy 8s removes that pause.

There is no discussion, no mid-round critique, and no chance to refine. You sketch, move on, and keep going. The time pressure pushes people past their first instinct and into ideas they would not normally reach.

## The Eight-Box Template

| Box | Example Prompt | Example Idea |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | How do we make users more active? | Add check-in rewards |
| 2 | How do we improve retention? | Personalized recommendations |
| 3 | How do we improve UX? | Simplify the flow |
| 4 | How do we increase revenue? | Membership subscriptions |
| 5 | How do we raise brand awareness? | Social media marketing |
| 6 | How do we improve the product? | An AI assistant |
| 7 | How do we raise customer satisfaction? | 24-hour support |
| 8 | How do we grow market share? | A partner program |

## When to Use It

Crazy 8s is best for divergence:

- Team brainstorms
- Solo warm-up exercises
- Early product design
- Any situation where one dominant idea should not take over

## GV x Slack Sprint

In 2014, Slack's early team and Google Ventures used a Design Sprint to explore the future of workplace communication. During the Crazy 8s round, one participant sketched a bot that would greet new users and guide them to send their first message.

That quick sketch later became Slackbot's welcome experience. The point was not that the drawing was beautiful. The point was that a rough sketch could unlock a much better product discussion.

## Six Keys to Make It Work

1. Frame the problem clearly. Ask a specific question.
2. Use time pressure as a tool, not a threat.
3. Favor quantity before quality.
4. Do not judge during the sketching round.
5. Think visually with symbols, words, and rough shapes.
6. Share the results quickly so the group can build on them.
