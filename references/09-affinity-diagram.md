## When Should You Use It?

Affinity diagrams are best when you are staring at a lot of messy data and do not know where to start. It is a visual organizing tool that categorizes scattered information and uses grouping to reveal patterns and relationships.

### Six Usage Scenarios

**Card 1 - Organizing user feedback**
- When you have dozens of user comments, how do you group them and extract the insight that really matters?

**Card 2 - Post-brainstorm cleanup**
- Brainstorming creates too many ideas; how do you turn them into an actionable plan?

**Card 3 - Team alignment**
- In cross-functional discussions, how do you bring different viewpoints together and build consensus?

**Card 4 - Root cause analysis**
- List and group all possible causes of a complex problem so you can identify the key drivers.

**Card 5 - Product feature planning**
- User feedback, user needs, and internal ideas get mixed together; how do you turn them into a clear roadmap?

**Card 6 - New project kickoff**
- For a new project or market, how do you structure the information so the team has a foundation to work from?

## One Whiteboard, Countless Sticky Notes

### Three Supporting Cards

**Card A - Step 1: Divergent thinking**
- Write all ideas, problems, and observations on sticky notes without judging them.

**Card B - Step 2: Organize**
- Group similar sticky notes together so natural clusters appear.

**Card C - Step 3: Name and summarize**
- Give each group a short title that captures its central theme.

## From Anthropological Field Notes to Digital Whiteboards

Affinity diagramming began as a practical way for a Japanese anthropologist to organize field notes. It evolved from physical sticky notes to digital tools, but it has always remained a visual way to understand complex problems deeply.

### Timeline

**1960s - Jiro Kawakita invents the method**
Japanese anthropologist Jiro Kawakita faced information overload while organizing field notes and developed a grouping method for writing ideas on cards.

**1987 - 3M formally names it**
3M named it the Affinity Diagram and included it in its Six Sigma toolkit.

**2000s - Digital tools emerge**
As tools like Miro and Figma grew, affinity diagrams moved from physical notes to digital whiteboards for remote collaboration.

**2024 - AI-assisted analysis becomes a trend**
AI tools can now identify themes and patterns in text automatically, speeding up affinity diagram creation.

## Five Steps from Chaos to Insight

The key principle of affinity diagramming is that clustering is a natural cognitive process. Observe patterns first, then intervene. Let the data speak for itself.

### Five Steps

**Step 1 - Prepare**
Collect all relevant information and write each idea on its own sticky note. Use different colors if you want to distinguish sources or types.

**Step 2 - Stick them up**
Place all sticky notes randomly on the wall or board. Do not arrange them in any order yet.

**Step 3 - Observe and group**
Look for similarities, relationships, and shared themes. Move notes into groups based on instinct, not pre-set categories.

**Step 4 - Name the categories**
Give each group a short title that captures the core idea or theme.

**Step 5 - Analyze and act**
Study each category, identify the most important ones, and turn the insights into a concrete action plan.
