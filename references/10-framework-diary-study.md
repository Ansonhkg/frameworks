# Diary Study

Let users record their experience continuously in real life, for a few days or for several weeks. Capture the context that a lab cannot reproduce and understand how behavior changes over time.

## Core View

### The lab cannot capture real life

Traditional user research has a fundamental limitation: it happens in a short time and in a controlled space, not in the ongoing situations where real behavior unfolds. Products are only seen through fragments of user behavior, and memory is unreliable - people may embellish, simplify, or completely forget difficult moments.

The lab can control variables with test groups and collect standardized responses through surveys, but those results often diverge from what people actually do in the real world. A diary study lets users record behavior over a day or several weeks in their natural environment.

The main value of a diary study is capture. It gives you the user's own account of behavior change, emotional swings, and decision paths - a continuous story that labs and surveys cannot reconstruct.

## Method Framework

### Five stages, about two weeks

A diary study is not just a matter of assigning a task and waiting. It is a continuous research process with five connected stages.

| Stage | Name | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| 01 | Define the scope and cadence | Decide what to capture, how often to record, and how long the diary should run (3-7 days). |
| 02 | Recruit and screen participants | Use the persona as a filter and confirm willingness and ability through a short interview. |
| 03 | Provide tools and training | Choose the format - app, paper notebook, voice notes, etc. - and train users to record honestly. |
| 04 | Follow up and motivate | Send reminders and support every day or every two days to keep participation high and answer questions quickly. |
| 05 | Collect and analyze data | Organize text, images, and audio into themes and synthesize insights. |

### Comparison Table

| Dimension | Diary Study | Lab Test | User Interview |
|----------|-------------|----------|----------------|
| Time span | Several days to several weeks (continuous) | 60-90 minutes (single session) | 45-60 minutes (single session) |
| Context realism | High (natural setting) | Low (controlled environment) | Medium (recall bias) |
| Behavior recording | Real-time or near-real-time recording | Observation + task completion | User narration (retrospective) |
| Best for | Habits, emotions, long-term change | Usability, task efficiency | Attitudes, motivation, decision logic |
| Main challenges | Diary fatigue, participant drop-off | Hawthorne effect, high cost | Memory bias, social desirability bias |

## When Should You Choose a Diary Study?

When your research question involves long-term experience, real situations, or behavior that is difficult for users to explain directly, a diary study is often the best method.

### Use Case Matrix

| Icon | Title | Description |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 📱 | Understand habits and behavior patterns | Sleep, diet, commuting, and other everyday scenarios where users may not remember accurately |
| 💡 | Track emotional and satisfaction change | Study how delight, frustration, and loyalty evolve while using a product |
| 🏠 | Understand real-life context | See how products fit into home environments, such as smart home devices |
| 🔍 | Explore hard-to-express experiences | Capture subtle emotional reactions to ads or micro-interactions in real time |
| 📊 | Study complex decision processes | Buying a home, buying a car, job hunting - behavior that unfolds over days or weeks |
| 🎯 | Capture implicit needs and pain points | Discover what users want but cannot clearly explain in words |

### Stage 1: Diary task design (M1)

- Recording dimensions: time, place, people, content
- Trigger types: event, emotion, scenario, POI
- Recording formats: text, image, audio, video
- Prompt types: why, how, feeling, suggestion
- Privacy and consent explanation

### Stage 2: Study design (M2)

- M1 diary task design -> M2 participant recruitment -> M3 data collection and analysis

### Stage 3: Data analysis framework (M3)

| Dimension | Metric | Analysis Method |
|------|------|----------|
| Behavior patterns | Usage duration, frequency, time distribution | Time-series analysis, clustering |
| Emotional tendency | Positive / negative / neutral ratio | NLP sentiment analysis, manual coding |
| Scenario association | Share of home / commute / work / leisure scenarios | Cross-tab analysis, heatmap visualization |

### Research Cycle Principle

- M1-M2: diary task design and recruitment (3-5 days)
- M2-M3: data collection and analysis (7-10 days)
- Keep the total cycle within 14 days to avoid fatigue and quality decline

## Five Principles

### Five rules that make diary studies work

The quality of a diary study depends on whether you are willing to spend enough real time, not on how many samples you can collect.

**01 Make the task specific, actionable, and low burden**
> "How did you feel when you used the app today?" is too vague. "Record the moments when you used the app most often, the features you liked and disliked most, and your emotional state at the time" is better.

**02 Keep the sample size modest (15-30 people) and do not chase statistical significance**
> The value of a diary study is depth, not breadth. More samples raise execution cost and reduce quality.

**03 The middle check-in (day 3-5) is the golden window for catching issues**
> A quick follow-up after three days helps you see whether the task is too hard or needs adjustment.

**04 Combine quantitative and qualitative analysis**
> Use qualitative analysis as the core and supplement it with quantitative indicators.

**05 Watch for "diary fatigue" - do not make the study too long or too frequent**
> Users participate to share experience, not to finish homework. A period longer than two weeks or more than three records a day will reduce engagement.

## Historical Evolution

### From psychology to HCI: the diary study's cross-disciplinary journey

The academic roots of the diary study go back to late 19th and early 20th century psychology experiments. Later, HCI researchers reinterpreted and systematized it.

| Year | Event / Figure | Description |
|------|----------------|-------------|
| 1900s | Early diary study forms | Psychologists began using self-observation diaries to record emotional changes, but the method had no formal framework. |
| 1950s | Psychology experiments standardize | The American Psychological Association introduced the idea of naturalistic behavior recording. |
| 1980s | HCI adopts it | As personal computers spread, researchers began studying how people use products in everyday life. |
| 2000s | Mobile devices change the method | Smartphones moved diary studies from paper to digital, making real-time logging possible. |
| 2010s | AI and big data combine | Machine learning can now analyze large diary datasets and reveal hidden patterns. |

## Companion Tools

### Tools that pair well with diary studies

- Phone audio/video recording - quickly capture immediate emotion and context
- Handwritten notebook - good for participants who prefer reflective writing
- Online collaborative docs such as Notion - useful for multi-person research projects
- Mind-mapping tools like XMind or Miro - help organize complex insight relationships
