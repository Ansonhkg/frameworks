# RICE Priority Model

RICE is Intercom's prioritization framework. It scores ideas by Reach, Impact, Confidence, and Effort so teams can stop arguing from instinct and start ranking work with a shared logic.

## Why It Exists

Prioritization meetings often turn into politics.

One team says a feature is important. Another says technical debt is urgent. The PM is stuck in the middle. RICE gives everyone a common language so those conversations become measurable instead of emotional.

## The Formula

RICE = Reach x Impact x Confidence / Effort

| Dimension | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Reach | How many users or customers will be affected in a given period? |
| Impact | How much value will each affected user get? |
| Confidence | How certain are you about the Reach, Impact, and Effort estimates? |
| Effort | How much team time is required to ship it? |

## Example

| Feature | Reach | Impact | Confidence | Effort | RICE Score |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| New feature A | 10,000 | 3 | 90% | 2 | 135,000 |
| Performance fix B | 5,000 | 2 | 80% | 1 | 80,000 |
| Bug fix C | 2,000 | 3 | 95% | 0.5 | 114,000 |

## When to Use It

RICE is best for prioritizing features when the team has limited resources and multiple competing requests.

## Why Intercom Used It

Intercom used RICE to reduce internal prioritization chaos.

- Decisions moved from opinion to data
- Different departments could discuss work in the same language
- Priorities became transparent instead of hidden

## Five Principles

1. Make Reach specific and measurable.
2. Use a consistent Impact scale instead of ad hoc scoring.
3. Be honest about Confidence.
4. Include hidden effort such as testing and deployment.
5. Revisit scores regularly and learn from outcomes.

## Limitations

- It is not a good fit for urgent work such as security fixes or compliance issues.
- It depends on good data.
- It does not fully capture long-term strategic alignment.
