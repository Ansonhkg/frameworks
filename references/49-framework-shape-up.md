# Shape Up

Shape Up is Basecamp's product development method. It uses fixed time and floating scope: the timebox is fixed, and the team decides what fits inside it.

## Why Scrum Backlogs Never End

Scrum backlog systems tend to grow forever. Story points become a currency, velocity becomes a number to chase, and scope keeps expanding.

Shape Up flips the model:

- Time is fixed, usually six weeks
- Scope is flexible
- The team chooses what fits and stops when the cycle ends

The workflow has three parts:

- Shaping
- Betting Table

Building

## Fixed Time, Floating Scope

Shape Up uses a six-week build cycle and a two-week cooldown.

During shaping, product people or designers turn an idea into a pitch. The pitch is not a detailed spec. It is just clear enough for the team to understand the scope and decide whether to take it.

## How Shape Up Works

| Stage | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Shaping | Define the rough shape of the work |
| Betting Table | Decide what is worth spending a cycle on |
| Building | Work on the chosen project without daily ceremony |
| Cool-down | Handle maintenance, bugs, and follow-up work |

## The Core Question

Shape Up asks a different question from Scrum:

- Not "how long will this take?"
- But "how much time is this worth?"

That shift changes the conversation from estimates to appetite.

## When to Use It

Shape Up works best for small teams shipping meaningful product work with a lot of autonomy.

It is a good fit when:

- You want a strong timebox and a real stop point
- You do not want a permanent backlog pulling the team apart
- You prefer shaping work before building it
- You want the team to own the project end to end

## Basecamp and HEY

Basecamp and HEY are the best-known Shape Up examples.

The team used appetite rather than estimates to decide whether a pitch deserved a six-week cycle. If the work did not fit, it was not forced into the schedule.

## Five Common Pitfalls

1. Treating the timebox as a hard deadline.
2. Skipping shaping and jumping straight into building.
3. Keeping a traditional backlog instead of a betting table.
4. Turning the cycle into a meeting-heavy agile process.
5. Treating the Hill Chart as a burn-down chart.
6. Using Shape Up for maintenance work it was not designed for.
