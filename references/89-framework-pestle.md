# PESTLE Analysis

PESTLE analysis scans six macro-environmental forces that can affect a business: Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, and Environmental.

## Why It Matters

Many products fail because of external change, not just weak execution. PESTLE helps teams look beyond the product itself and notice the forces that can reshape the market.

## The Six Dimensions

| Letter | Dimension | Typical Factors |
|---|---|---|
| P | Political | Policy changes, trade restrictions, tax rules |
| E | Economic | GDP growth, interest rates, exchange rates, inflation |
| S | Social | Demographics, education, lifestyle, values |
| T | Technological | New technologies, pace of innovation, R&D intensity |
| L | Legal | Labor law, IP law, regulation |
| E | Environmental | Climate change, sustainability pressure, environmental rules |

## When to Use It

- Before entering a new market
- When launching a new product line
- During annual strategy planning
- When growth slows without an obvious internal cause
- Before fundraising, IPO, or M&A

## Netflix in China

Netflix's attempted entry into China showed how six forces can all work against a company at once.

| Dimension | What Went Wrong |
|---|---|
| Political | Content approval and licensing barriers |
| Economic | Price expectations and weak subscription habits |
| Social | Local content preferences and different media habits |
| Technological | Network quality and device ecosystem differences |
| Legal | Copyright and compliance complexity |
| Environmental | Intense local platform competition |

## How to Do a Deep PESTLE Analysis

1. Define the exact market or customer segment.
2. Collect data for each dimension.
3. Look at future trends, not just the current state.
4. Rate the likely impact of each trend.
5. Build response options for the highest-risk items.

## Why Many PESTLE Analyses Waste Time

| Problem | Why It Hurts |
|---|---|
| Outdated data | Macro conditions change quickly |
| Separate silos | The dimensions affect each other |
| No quantification | Vague statements do not support decisions |
| Oversimplification | Important variables get lost |
| Ignoring weak signals | The most important change is often not the most obvious one |
