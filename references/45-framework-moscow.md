# MoSCoW Method

MoSCoW is a simple prioritization method that splits requirements into Must have, Should have, Could have, and Won't have. It helps teams agree on what matters most and control scope creep.

## Why Sprints Slip

Sprint planning often turns into a tug-of-war. Product wants more. Engineering says the timeline is already tight. MoSCoW helps by forcing every request to answer one question: if we do not build this, does the product fail?

## The Four Buckets

| Bucket | Meaning | Typical Share |
|---|---|---|
| Must have | The product cannot ship or deliver core value without it | 60% |
| Should have | Important, but it can be delayed a little | 20% |
| Could have | Nice to have, but not essential | 10% |
| Won't have | Deliberately not included this time | 10% |

Key idea: the Won't have list matters. It gives the team permission to stop discussing work that is not in scope.

## When to Use It

MoSCoW is useful whenever a team needs a fast, shared view of priority:

- Sprint planning
- MVP definition
- Pre-launch requirement sorting
- Working under resource constraints
- Negotiating with stakeholders
- Backlog grooming

## GOV.UK Case Study

The UK Government Digital Service used MoSCoW when rebuilding GOV.UK and consolidating hundreds of government websites into one platform.

The team focused on only the essential work, which helped them ship quickly and avoid the endless scope expansion that often affects government IT projects.

### Why it worked

- Must-have items stopped the team from debating everything
- The method made the MVP boundary clear
- The team kept each iteration focused

## Five Keys

1. Keep Must have below about 50-60 percent.
2. Involve engineering so the priority list is feasible.
3. Reassess priorities as context changes.
4. Treat Won't have as a real decision, not a forgotten pile.
5. Use MoSCoW as a framework, not as the final answer to every conflict.
