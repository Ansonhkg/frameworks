# System Archetypes

System archetypes are recurring structures behind common organizational problems. Peter Senge used the term to describe patterns that repeat across teams, companies, and industries.

## Why Solutions Make Problems Worse

Many problems look unique on the surface, but the same few structures appear again and again. Once you recognize the structure, you can stop reacting like a firefighter and start changing the system itself.

## Six Common Archetypes

| Archetype | What It Means | Typical Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Fixes That Fail | A short-term fix creates a worse long-term problem | Quick relief, then deeper damage |
| Limits to Growth | Growth hits an invisible constraint | Expansion slows when the system saturates |
| Shifting the Burden | Symptom relief replaces root-cause work | Short-term relief weakens long-term capability |
| Drifting Goals | Standards are lowered to match reality | Performance slips as expectations fall |
| Escalation | Two sides keep reacting to each other | Competition becomes a spiral |
| Tragedy of the Commons | Shared resources are overused | Everyone loses as the commons degrades |

## When to Use It

Use system archetypes when the same problem keeps returning, when a fast fix seems to work but later backfires, or when one solution creates another problem.

Typical examples:

- Repeated sales declines after aggressive promotion
- Fast growth that suddenly stalls
- Price wars and ad wars
- Lowering standards to avoid short-term pressure
- Shared resources such as budget, staff, or servers being overused

## MySpace Case Study

MySpace is a classic example of a short-term fix that made the underlying problem worse.

### What Happened

- As Facebook grew, MySpace lost users
- The company responded by increasing ads and commercial pressure
- User experience got worse
- User loss accelerated instead of slowing down

### Simplified Timeline

| Year | Monthly Active Users | Ad Revenue Share | User Experience Score |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2006 | 100 million | 35% | 4.5/5 |
| 2007 | 80 million | 45% | 3.8/5 |
| 2008 | 50 million | 60% | 2.9/5 |
| 2009 | 20 million | 75% | 1.8/5 |

### What This Shows

- The short-term fix was to increase ad revenue
- The side effect was a worse product experience
- The missing response was product innovation and trust-building

## How to Apply System Archetypes

1. Describe the repeating pattern in one sentence.
2. Draw the causal loop and connect the key variables.
3. Match the pattern to a known archetype.
4. Find the leverage point that can break the loop.
5. Design an intervention that changes the structure, not just the symptoms.
