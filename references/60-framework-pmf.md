# Product-Market Fit

Product-Market Fit is the point where users genuinely want the product and growth starts to make sense. Before PMF, growth is usually an illusion.

## Why PMF Matters

You can spend money on marketing, hiring, and infrastructure, but none of that fixes the core problem if people do not really need the product.

Marc Andreessen popularized the term in 2007. The big question is simple: have we actually found PMF?

## How to Measure It

The most common measurement is Sean Ellis's survey question:

> How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?

| Response | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Very disappointed | Strong PMF signal |
| Somewhat disappointed | Some value, but not strong enough |
| Not disappointed | Weak or no PMF |
| I already stopped using it | No PMF signal |

If roughly 40% of respondents say they would be very disappointed, that is often treated as a strong PMF signal.

## When to Use It

PMF is useful when:

- A product has just launched
- A major feature has shipped
- Growth has slowed or stalled
- You are preparing to raise money

## Superhuman

Superhuman became a well-known PMF case because Rahul Vohra built a rigorous PMF measurement process around user feedback.

The idea was not just to ask whether people liked the product. It was to identify the segment that loved it most and double down on that segment.

## How to Make PMF Measurement Useful

1. Ask the right survey question.
2. Segment responses by user type.
3. Follow up with interviews.
4. Recheck the signal regularly.
5. Treat PMF as a milestone, not a finish line.
