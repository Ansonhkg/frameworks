# Hook Model

The Hook Model describes how products can become habit-forming through a repeated cycle of trigger, action, variable reward, and investment.

## Why It Matters

Most products rely on external marketing to bring people back.

Hook aims to create internal triggers so users return on their own, because the product has become part of their routine.

## The Four Steps

| Step | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Trigger | Something prompts the user to think of the product |
| Action | The user takes a simple action |
| Variable Reward | The outcome is rewarding, but not always predictable |
| Investment | The user puts something into the product, making the next loop stronger |

## Trigger

- External triggers: notifications, email, ads, links
- Internal triggers: feelings such as boredom, loneliness, anxiety, or curiosity

## Action

Hook uses the idea that a behavior happens when motivation, ability, and a prompt align.

The action must be simple enough that the user can do it quickly.

## Variable Reward

The reward should not feel exactly the same every time.

Three common reward types:

1. Social reward - likes, comments, recognition
2. Hunt reward - the thrill of discovering something interesting
3. Self reward - the satisfaction of completing something

## Investment

The more a user invests, the more likely they are to come back.

Examples:

- Posting a photo
- Following someone
- Writing a comment
- Editing a profile

## Where It Works Best

Hook is most effective in products used frequently, such as:

- Social networks and messaging apps
- Shopping apps with limited-time deals
- Travel apps with price alerts
- News apps
- Games with daily rewards
- Finance and habit-tracking apps

## Instagram Example

Instagram is a classic Hook case.

- Trigger: notifications and the internal urge to check what others are doing
- Action: open the app, browse, like, and post
- Variable reward: unpredictable content and social feedback
- Investment: photos, follows, comments, and saved content

The result was a product that became part of daily habit instead of a tool users opened only when prompted.

## Five Principles

1. Solve a real user problem.
2. Find the user's internal trigger.
3. Make the action extremely simple.
4. Make the reward variable and satisfying.
5. Increase user investment over time.
