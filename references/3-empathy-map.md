# Empathy Map

A user-centered tool: what people say is not the whole story, what they do is not the whole story, and what they feel is where design becomes real.

## When Should You Use It?

An empathy map is useful when you already have research data but have not yet turned it into insight. It helps the team organize raw material into a shared understanding of the user.

### Common Scenarios

1. Customer journey analysis: turn large interview logs and multi-source data into a user-centered picture.
2. New product or prototype work: use the map during product design and development to understand user needs and usage context.
3. Marketing strategy: understand what the target audience thinks, feels, and truly needs.
4. Internationalization and localization: better understand user needs and preferences across cultures and regions.
5. After-sales service: understand the full post-purchase journey and deliver targeted support.

## Four Quadrants, One Complete Person

The empathy map breaks the user into four dimensions: Says, Thinks, Does, and Feels. Only when all four are combined do you get a complete picture of the person.

### Says

- Direct statements from the user
- Verbatim quotes from an interview
- "This feature is very useful to me."
- "I would buy it if it were cheaper."

### Thinks

- Thoughts the user may not say out loud
- "Will this company misuse my personal data?"
- "Do I really need to pay for this?"
- "Is there a cheaper alternative?"

### Does

- Observable behavior
- Uses only 20% of the product's core features
- Gives up as soon as a problem appears
- Opens several similar apps at once

### Feels

- Emotional reactions and inner experience
- Feels anxious about the learning curve
- Feels uneasy after data loss
- Feels confused by a complex interface

### Notes

**Pain**
- Complex features and a steep learning curve
- Concerns about personal privacy and data leaks
- Difficulty finding the core content quickly

**Gain**
- Fast onboarding and immediate feedback
- A one-stop data integration experience
- Efficient completion of daily work tasks

## From Workshop Tool to Design Standard

The empathy map originated in Dave Gray's workshop practice. After *Gamestorming* was published, it spread quickly and became one of the most common user-understanding tools in design work.

### Timeline

**2000**
**Early workshop tool**
Dave Gray developed this framework while creating visual thinking techniques for enterprise workshops. It helped teams extract patterns from fragmented information and align quickly on direction.

**2010**
**Formally named in *Gamestorming***
Dave Gray, Bill Burnett, and James Murnane published *Gamestorming*, which helped establish the empathy map as a standard tool in training and innovation consulting.

**2015**
**IDEO.org brings it into social innovation**
IDEO.org introduced the empathy map to user research in international humanitarian and development projects, proving its value across cultures and problem domains.

**2017**
**Adopted in Silicon Valley growth work**
Early Silicon Valley teams used the map as a standard tool for growth, UX, and "say/think/do/feel" analysis.

**2020**
**Integrated into digital whiteboards like Miro**
With remote collaboration, the empathy map became a common template on Miro and other design boards used by product teams worldwide.

## How Microsoft Redesigned the Xbox Adaptive Controller

Before designing the Xbox Adaptive Controller, Microsoft's design team worked with organizations such as Warfighter Engaged and built detailed empathy maps for players with limited mobility. The maps helped the team realize that users did not need something more complex or more powerful; they needed a more accessible way to play.

### Xbox Adaptive Controller
**Xbox Adaptive Controller - Empathy Map | 2017-2019**

#### Says
> "I usually play with one hand, so I cannot operate it like everyone else."
> "I want a controller that supports one-handed play."

#### Thinks
> "I worry that I cannot move as freely as other players."
> "I want someone to understand my limitations and make me feel included."

#### Does
> "I use one side of the controller to play."
> "I ask someone else to connect the device because the buttons are hard to press."
> "I place the controller on my lap to reduce strain and fatigue."

#### Feels
> "I want the adaptive controller to be simple and not complicated."
> "I want the controller to support one-handed use."
> "I do not want to be treated differently. I want to feel like a real player."

#### Pain
> "Because of limited hand mobility, I cannot move as freely as other players."
> "I want someone to understand my limitations and make me feel included."

#### Gain
> "A simpler controller lets me focus on actually playing."
> "Fast, immediate feedback is critical for learning the game."
> "I feel proud when I use a truly accessible controller."

#### Notes
> "Games are how I connect with people and share joy."
> "If I cannot play, I need support to stay involved."
> "A truly accessible controller makes me feel more valued."

### Design Insight and Improvement

The starting point was the needs of disabled veterans. The empathy map showed that users did not want a more complex or higher-performance controller; they needed a more inclusive way to play. That pushed the product team to rethink the entire design philosophy, and the final Xbox Adaptive Controller supported more than 20 expansion ports so every player could find a setup that worked for them.

## How to Enter the User's Mind

The key to filling out an empathy map is distinguishing between what users say and what they do. The first two quadrants should be based on real data; the last two are informed inferences. The result should be a set of testable hypotheses.

### Step 1: Define the target user

Choose a specific user group and focus on a concrete context or problem.

- Define your target audience: who is the primary subject?
- Distinguish between user, consumer, and buyer so you know who makes the real decision
- Fill it out as a group to avoid a single-point perspective
- Use typical behavioral assumptions from the persona as a starting point, then verify them later

### Step 2: Collect raw data

Gather real feedback and behavioral notes through interviews, observation, surveys, and social media.

### Step 3: Fill in Says and Does

Put the collected information into the appropriate quadrants and separate verbal statements from observed behavior.

### Step 4: Infer Thinks and Feels

Use the data and observations to infer likely thoughts and emotional states.

### Step 5: Summarize pain and gain

Extract the key insights from the four quadrants and turn them into a deeper understanding of needs and expectations.

- Distinguish between surface needs, stated product requests, and the real underlying value
- Thinks and Feels reveal hidden motivation and are not fully expressed on the surface
- Pay attention to implicit needs and emotional drivers
- Avoid over-interpreting or missing subtle clues in user language

### Step 6: Validate the hypotheses

Turn the Says/Thinks/Does/Feels insights into testable hypotheses and validate them through later user feedback and data analysis.
