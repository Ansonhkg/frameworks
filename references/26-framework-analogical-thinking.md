# Analogical Thinking

Analogical thinking helps you break out of domain-specific thinking by borrowing patterns from one field and applying them to another. It is one of the most useful ways to trigger cross-disciplinary innovation.

## The Invisible Cage of Domain Thinking

Product people often think inside frameworks, but the framework boundary is also the boundary of innovation. The strongest ideas often appear when designers, engineers, and domain experts connect ideas across fields.

Analogical thinking is the bridge. It shifts the mind from narrow vertical expertise to broader lateral connection. Instead of asking only how to optimize a codebase, you can ask how a logistics network, a restaurant kitchen, or an aircraft system handles the same kind of pressure.

## Four Types of Analogy

| Type | Name | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Direct analogy | Borrow a solution from another domain | Use Amazon's logistics logic to design a delivery system |
| 2 | Personal analogy | Put yourself inside the problem | If I were a drop of water, what resistance would I meet in a pipe? |
| 3 | Symbolic analogy | Use an abstract image to express the essence | Treat data flow like a river and look for the narrowest channel |
| 4 | Fantasy analogy | Break reality and imagine the ideal state | What if a phone could change shape like water? |

Direct analogy is the most common. Fantasy analogy is the one most likely to produce a breakthrough.

## Choosing the Right Type

| Dimension | Direct Analogy | Personal Analogy |
|---|---|---|
| Best for | Problems with mature solutions elsewhere | Problems that require deep experiential understanding |
| Example use | Logistics, operations, process design | UX design, service design |
| Innovation level | Medium | High |

| Dimension | Symbolic Analogy | Fantasy Analogy |
|---|---|---|
| Best for | Abstract or essence-level problems | Breakthrough innovation and zero-to-one ideas |
| Example use | Branding, culture, positioning | New product concepts, disruptive technologies |
| Innovation level | High | Very high |

Quick guide:

- Existing pattern available: use direct analogy
- Experience problem: use personal analogy
- Abstract problem: use symbolic analogy
- Breakthrough problem: use fantasy analogy

## When to Use It

Analogical thinking is useful for:

- Product innovation and feature breakthroughs
- Organizational change and management improvement
- Data analysis and pattern discovery
- Marketing and brand positioning

It is not copy and paste. It is mapping and translation across domains.

## iPhone Touch Physics and the First Apple Mouse

Steve Jobs needed the iPhone to make a glass screen respond with the precision of a physical button. The engineering challenge was obvious, but the solution came from analogy: use a mechanism that amplifies small physical input into a larger response.

The same pattern shaped the first Apple mouse. Jobs wanted a mouse that moved naturally, like a human hand. IDEO used that prompt to design a simple one-button mouse that became a standard reference point for later mouse design.

The value of the analogy was not decoration. It connected two unrelated fields and opened a new path to the answer.

## Five Keys

1. Build a broad knowledge base by reading across disciplines.
2. Pay close attention to patterns in daily life and in nature.
3. Practice cross-domain thinking regularly.
4. Keep a personal analogy toolbox of examples and references.
5. Apply and reflect continuously so the skill improves over time.

Analogical thinking is trainable. It is not just a talent.

## Academic Roots

The idea has ancient roots, but modern theory developed through 20th-century scholarship.

| Year | Milestone |
|---|---|
| 1957 | I. A. Richards systematizes analogy theory in *The Philosophy of Rhetoric* |
| 1963 | Kenneth Burke extends analogy into social analysis |
| 1976 | George Lakoff and Mark Johnson publish *Metaphors We Live By* |
| 1989 | Donald Schon applies analogy to professional education in *The Reflective Practitioner* |
| 2001 | Douglas Hofstadter publishes *Fluid Concepts and Creative Analogies* |

### Research evolution

| Era | Main approach | Representative scholars |
|---|---|---|
| 1950s-60s | Philosophical reasoning | Richards, Burke |
| 1970s-80s | Linguistic analysis | Lakoff, Johnson |
| 1990s-2000s | Cognitive psychology experiments | Gentner, Holyoak |
| 2010s-present | Neuroscience and AI | Gauthier, Mitchell |

### Current applications

- AI: analogy-based reasoning is important in machine learning
- Education: analogy is widely used in STEM teaching
- Business: design thinking and blue-ocean strategy both depend on analogical transfer
