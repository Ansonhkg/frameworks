# Innovation Diffusion Curve

The Innovation Diffusion Curve describes how a product spreads from early adopters to the mainstream. Adoption usually starts slowly, accelerates, and then levels off into saturation.

## Why Early Success Does Not Guarantee Mainstream Success

Early users are not the same as the mass market. A product can win over innovators and early adopters while still failing to cross the gap into broad adoption.

## Five Types of Adopters

| Group | Share | What They Look Like |
|---|---|---|
| Innovators | 2.5% | First to try new ideas |
| Early Adopters | 13.5% | Opinion leaders and enthusiasts |
| Early Majority | 34% | Pragmatic mainstream users |
| Late Majority | 34% | Cautious users who wait for proof |
| Laggards | 16% | The last to adopt |

The most dangerous gap is the chasm between early adopters and the early majority. Crossing it usually requires a product that is simple, credible, and clearly useful.

## When to Use It

- To understand product adoption stages
- To plan launch strategy and positioning
- To decide whether a product is ready for the mainstream
- To design marketing for different adopter groups

## Salesforce Case Study

Salesforce launched one of the first SaaS CRM products in 1999. It entered a market dominated by expensive on-premise systems such as Siebel.

### Why It Worked

- Lower implementation friction
- Subscription pricing instead of large upfront licensing
- Faster deployment
- A cleaner product story for smaller teams

### What the Case Shows

Salesforce did not just build a product. It crossed the chasm by making the offer easier to adopt than the incumbent alternative.

## How to Apply the Curve

1. Identify which adopter group you are currently serving.
2. Check whether the product story matches that group's needs.
3. Look for the chasm between early adopters and the mainstream.
4. Adapt the product, pricing, and message for the next group.
5. Measure adoption rate over time, not just initial interest.
