# The Five-Question Interview Method

A good interview is not casual conversation. Every follow-up brings you one step closer to the truth.

**Title**: The Five-Question Interview Method  
**Tags**: User research, product management, user experience  
**Date**: January 15, 2024

## When Should You Use It?

The five-question interview method is useful whenever you need to understand real user motivation and context in depth. Its core is to dig from surface-level symptoms down into the hidden needs and thoughts behind them.

### Four Common Use Cases

**Product research stage**  
Before launch or during feature iteration, use the method to understand how users actually use the product and where the pain points are.

**Competitive research**  
When comparing competitors, use the questions to understand why users chose them and where your product can differentiate.

**UX optimization**  
For a specific feature or workflow, use the method to uncover what users really feel so you know what to improve.

**Prioritization**  
When resources are limited, use the method to decide which needs are truly frequent and valuable instead of being misled by surface requests.

## From Broad to Deep Funnel Questions

This framework organizes interviews into five levels, from the broadest background questions to the deepest motivation questions. Each level has a clear goal, a good time to ask it, and a specific set of clues that help build a pyramid-like understanding of the user.

### Level 1: Understand the user profile
- **Goal:** Build a basic mental model
- **Sample questions:** "What industry do you work in?" "What is your role?"
- **Key elements:** Job background, usage context, basic information

### Level 2: Describe specific behavior
- **Goal:** Reconstruct the real usage scenario
- **Sample questions:** "How do you usually use this feature?" "When was the last time?"
- **Key elements:** Time, frequency, action path

### Level 3: Explore motivation
- **Goal:** Understand why the behavior happens
- **Sample questions:** "Why did you choose that instead of this?" "What made you start using this feature?"
- **Key elements:** Trigger points, decision factors

### Level 4: Dig into details
- **Goal:** Get deeper insight
- **Sample questions:** "Can you describe what happened at that moment?" "How was that decision made?"
- **Key elements:** Emotional factors, environmental factors, influence factors

### Level 5: Extract key conclusions
- **Goal:** Turn the conversation into actionable insight
- **Sample questions:** "If you had to choose again, what would you do?" "In what situation would you not use this feature?"
- **Key elements:** Core needs, pain point summary, opportunity points

## From Knowledge Research to Benefit Interviews

The five-question method is built on five levels of progression: Ask, Understand, Explore, Validate, and Summarize. Repeating those levels creates a full user insight report.

### Timeline

**1960s - Cognitive Research Era**
- Representative figures: Herbert Simon, Daniel Kahneman
- Main methods: surveys, in-depth interviews
- Characteristic: focus on explicit needs and surface behavior

**1980s - Contextual Design emerges**
- Representative figures: Hugh Beyer, Karen Holtzblatt
- Main methods: contextual inquiry, participatory observation
- Characteristic: understand behavior in the real context

**2000s - Jobs to Be Done appears**
- Representative figures: Clayton Christensen, Anthony Ulwick
- Main methods: JTBD interviews
- Characteristic: focus on the job users hire products to do, not the feature itself

**2010s - Customer discovery matures**
- Representative figures: Steve Blank, Ash Maurya
- Main methods: customer development in lean startup
- Characteristic: rapid hypothesis validation and iterative learning

**2020s - The method becomes systematized**
- Blends traditional user research with modern agile thinking into a structured interview framework
- Characteristic: deep follow-up and scenario reconstruction, suitable for product discovery and demand analysis

## How Slack Discovered the Real Value of Channels

Slack initially learned about the problem of information overload through user interviews, but the real understanding came from digging deeper with the five-question method. Here is a classic conversation from the Slack product team:

### Interview Transcript

**Q1: How do you usually use Slack?**  
A: "I open Slack every day and check the messages in each channel."

**Q2: Which feature do you use the most, and why?**  
A: "Probably direct messages, because sometimes I need to talk to a specific person."

**Q3: When do you choose channels instead of direct messages?**  
A: "When several people need to know the same thing."

**Q4: Can you be more specific about what kind of things?**  
A: "Project updates, team announcements, that kind of thing. But honestly, sometimes there are so many messages that I do not know which ones matter."

**Q5: If you could redesign Slack, how would you improve channels?**  
A: "I would organize channels by topic or project instead of mixing everything together."

### Key Insight

The five-question method helped Slack realize that the real need was not "more communication channels" but "a better organized way to communicate." That insight directly shaped Slack's later channel categories and topic labels.

## Five Layers, Closer to the Truth

The method's power is in moving step by step toward the user's real need and pain point. Each layer has a clear goal and a practical use.

### Layer 1: Understand the user profile
- **Goal:** Build a basic mental model
- **Key questions:** "What industry do you work in?" "What is your role?"
- **Use case:** Start the interview, break the ice, and build trust

### Layer 2: Describe specific behavior
- **Goal:** Reconstruct the real usage scenario
- **Key questions:** "How do you usually use this feature?" "When was the last time?"
- **Use case:** Understand actual usage

### Layer 3: Explore motivation
- **Goal:** Understand why the behavior happens
- **Key questions:** "Why did you choose that instead of this?" "What made you start using this feature?"
- **Use case:** Reveal the essence of the user's need

### Layer 4: Dig into details
- **Goal:** Get deeper insight
- **Key questions:** "Can you describe what happened at that moment?" "How was that decision made?"
- **Use case:** Understand the decision process

### Layer 5: Extract key conclusions
- **Goal:** Turn the conversation into actionable insight
- **Key questions:** "If you had to choose again, what would you do?" "In what situation would you not use this feature?"
- **Use case:** Summarize core needs and opportunity points

### Best Practices

1. Prepare questions in advance, but stay flexible during the interview.
2. Record the session so important details are not lost.
3. Organize notes and analysis within 24 hours.
4. Cross-check several interviews so one-off responses do not distort the results.
