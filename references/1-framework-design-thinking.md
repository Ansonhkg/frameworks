## What is Design Thinking?

### When Should You Use It?

Design thinking is not a tool exclusive to designers. Any scenario requiring understanding people, defining problems, and generating creative solutions can be driven by this framework—especially when the problem itself is unclear.

## Five-Step Cycle, Loop Back Anytime

The core of design thinking is a "human-centered" iterative process. The five stages are not executed linearly; if problems are discovered at any stage, you can return to the previous step until finding the optimal solution—not just the fastest or cheapest one.

### Five-Step Cycle Diagram (Left to Right)

**1. Empathize**
- Understand user problems
- Observe user behavior
- Interview users
- Experience the product

**2. Define**
- Organize user needs
- Extract core problems
- Determine design direction
- Establish goal framework

**3. Ideate**
- Brainstorm solutions
- Divergent thinking
- "Yes, and..."
- Quick prototype conception

**4. Prototype**
- Build low-cost models
- Visualize solutions
- Create simple tools
- Simulate real scenarios

**5. Test**
- Interact with real users
- Collect feedback and suggestions
- Validate assumptions and solutions
- Adjust, iterate, test again

---

**The Essence of Design Thinking**

Design thinking emphasizes "starting from understanding people," not from technology or business goals. It encourages teams to dive deep into user scenarios early on, discovering real needs through observation and interviews rather than relying on assumptions. This approach is especially suitable for innovation projects because traditional methods often overlook human complexity and emotional factors.

## From Academic Concept to Global Methodology

Design thinking was not a single invention by one person; it is the product of design studies, cognitive science, and engineering practice converging over more than half a century. It was brought to the global business world by IDEO's former leader Tim Brown.

### Timeline

**1960s | Herbert Simon "The Sciences of the Artificial" First Mentioned**
- Proposed the concept of "design thinking," but did not form a systematic methodology

**1959 | IDEO Founded**
- David Kelley founded IDEO, shifting design from aesthetics to problem-solving orientation

**2008 | Tim Brown Publishes HBR Article**
- The article "Design Thinking" brought this framework into mainstream business view

**2013 | Stanford d.school Promotion**
- Hasso Plattner Institute of Design (d.school) systematized teaching, becoming one of the standard courses in entrepreneurship education

## How IDEO × Bank of America Used Design Thinking to Invent "Keep the Change"

### Background Introduction
IDEO was commissioned by Bank of America to design a savings product for young customers. The five processes of design thinking were fully executed in this project, ultimately giving birth to one of the hit products: "round-up automatic savings."

---

### Bank of America × IDEO Case Study

**Time**: 2008

#### Step 1: Empathize
The IDEO team deeply observed young users' (mainly women) daily consumption, saving behaviors, and financial concepts, discovering a key pain point: "I know I should save money, but I always forget."

> **Insight Point**: Saving behavior is too active and requires willpower; while spending happens passively.

#### Step 2: Define
Reframed the problem from "How to make users more disciplined in saving?" to "How to make saving happen as naturally, imperceptibly, and painlessly as spending?"

> **Design Goal**: Make saving an automatic byproduct of consumption, not a separate decision.

#### Step 3: Ideate
The team proposed over 20 concepts, including smart ATM reminders (failed), automatic transfers (successful), gamified incentives, etc. Finally filtered "round-up savings" as the core solution.

> **Core Logic**: Every time a purchase is made, the bank automatically transfers the change to the savings account—imperceptible and painless for users.

#### Step 4: Prototype
Created paper mock interfaces and simple flowcharts, letting real users experience the "round-up savings" concept in real scenarios and collecting feedback.

> **Design Detail**: Through visual display of small-amount accumulation effects, let users feel the sense of achievement of "small money becoming big money."

#### Step 5: Test
Invited 20 households to participate in a 3-month real test, collecting behavioral data and emotional feedback. Over 90% of participants said "I didn't realize I was saving money, but my account balance increased by 14%." The product finally launched.

> **Test Result**: "Imperceptible savings" became the key factor for success; user retention far exceeded expectations.

## Five Steps Driven, Starting from People

Each step has clear deliverables; speed is not required, nor is "high completion." When you feel— the goal is that after completing the next step, the team's understanding of users is one level deeper than at the start.

### Detailed Explanation of Five Steps

| Step | Title | Description |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 01 | **Empathize · Understand Users** | Dive deep into scenarios, observe behaviors, listen to voices. Temporarily put aside your own assumptions and enter "the user's world." |
| 02 | **Define · Clarify Problems** | Extract key problems from insights, express them using the "How might we..." sentence structure, avoiding falling into the "why" trap. |
| 03 | **Ideate · Diverge Solutions** | Brainstorm without limits, pursue "quantity over quality." Encourage crazy ideas, delay judgment. |
| 04 | **Prototype · Build Quickly** | Make touchable solutions at minimum cost; the purpose is to validate assumptions, not perfect presentation. Can be sketches, storyboards, or simple models. |
| 05 | **Test · Learn from Feedback** | Let real users interact with prototypes, observe behaviors rather than just listening to opinions. Record "surprises" and "moments of confusion." |

---

### Core Principles of Design Thinking

- Rapidly iterate prototypes, finding optimal solutions through actual validation
- Start from the user's perspective, understanding their needs and expectations
- Allow failure, treating errors as learning opportunities rather than endpoints
- Encourage multidisciplinary teams—designers, engineers—to participate together

### Application Scenarios of Design Thinking

- Solving complex social problems such as poverty and educational inequality
- Innovative product development, full-process support from concept to market
- Internal enterprise process optimization, improving efficiency and employee satisfaction
- Government policy formulation, increasing policy acceptance through user participation
