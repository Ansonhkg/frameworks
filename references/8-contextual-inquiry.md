# Contextual Inquiry

## When Should You Use It?

Contextual inquiry is best when user research and user design need to stay tightly connected. It works especially well for work, professional tools, daily consumer behavior, and complex decision-making in specific life contexts.

### Best Fit Scenarios

**Customer work context**  
Use it when work includes multiple roles and interactions, and the environment is critical to understanding product impact.

**Team collaboration**  
Enterprise software or collaboration platforms need fast validation before development so they can solve real collaboration pain points.

**Research-driven products**  
When designing products for specific industries such as healthcare or finance, you have to go on site to understand hidden needs.

**Workflow design**  
For company software or B2B products, observing real work is the most direct way to find process problems.

**Cross-cultural product design**  
Global products need to account for regional habits and cultural factors that are hard to see from the outside.

## The Apprentice Relationship and Four Principles

The core metaphor of contextual inquiry is the "master-apprentice" relationship: the user is the master and the researcher is the apprentice. The apprentice learns in the user's work environment instead of pulling the user into a lab.

### Roles

**Master (observation)**
- In the real work environment
- Through direct observation

**Apprentice (researcher)**
- Listen, ask, and record
- Do not interrupt the user's workflow

### Four Principles

**CONTEXT**
Observe in the user's real work setting, not in a lab.

**PARTNERSHIP**
Work with the user instead of interviewing them in one direction only.

**INTERPRETATION**
Translate observed behavior into needs, with both sides discussing and confirming the meaning.

**FOCUS**
Enter the field with a clear objective so the research stays directional.

## From Anthropology Fieldwork to a User Research Method

Contextual inquiry emerged from Lucy Suchman's research on situated action theory, and Beyer and Holtzblatt turned it into a systemized UX research method through industrial practice.

### 1987
**Lucy Suchman - *Planned and Situated Actions***
While working at PARC, Lucy Suchman proposed situated action theory, arguing that people understand their environment through action rather than through a fully preplanned script. That insight became the theoretical base for contextual inquiry.

### 1988
**Beyer and Holtzblatt at DEC Labs**
Hugh Beyer and Karen Holtzblatt brought ethnographic methods into product design and developed the early form of contextual interview.

### 1990
**ACM SIGCHI publication**
Beyer and Holtzblatt formally published the method at an ACM interaction design conference, providing a practical toolkit for the research community.

### 1994
**Publication of *Contextual Design***
The book systematized contextual inquiry inside a larger contextual design process, from research to design, and became a classic UX textbook.

### 2007
**Agile and lean user research**
As agile development spread, contextual inquiry was adapted into lighter-weight practices such as RapidCI and Flash Studies that fit iterative workflows.

## Microsoft - Office 2003 Usability Crisis Study

As Office became more complex, Microsoft noticed that users often had to search for help, make mistakes, or fail to find features. Lab testing could not capture the real work environment, so Microsoft asked Beyer's team to enter users' actual offices and conduct a six-month deep study, which ultimately helped inspire the Ribbon interface in Office 2007.

### Microsoft Office
**Enterprise productivity software | Office 2003 usability contextual study**

#### Research Context
- **Timeline**: 4 months (September 2001 - January 2002)
- **Method**: Master-apprentice - researchers worked as apprentices at the user's desk

#### Core Findings

**Feature use and layout were poor**
More than 85% of features used by the 20% of users were hidden deep inside Office menus. Many buttons were never clicked, but users often had to search for them manually.

**The learning cost was too high**
"We spent a lot of time finding where the feature was instead of using it to work."

#### Research Conclusion

**The toolbar design was far from the user's mental model**
"Users could not understand why some features were here and others were there. The menu hierarchy had no clear logic, so finding things was unintuitive and error-prone."

#### Research Recommendations

**Redesign the toolbar and menu structure**
Put common features in more visible places and reduce memory load.

**Add visual guidance and hints**
Help users find the right entry points faster.

**Provide scenario-based help content**
Users should be able to understand the tool in real usage contexts, not just in abstract documentation.

## Five Steps to Run a Contextual Inquiry

Contextual inquiry is not simply "go to the user's home and watch." It requires preparation, skilled field interaction, and disciplined analysis afterward.

### The Five Steps

**Pre-planning**
Define the research goals, choose the right context and sample, and prepare a draft interview guide and observation focus.

**Enter the field**
Go into the user's workspace as an apprentice and record behavior, dialogue, and environment.

**Interactive learning**
Ask the user direct questions in the field: "Why do it this way?" "How does this button work?" "What was that step for?"

**Record key behaviors**
Listen, observe, and write field notes about actions, dialogue, and environmental changes.

**Analyze and synthesize**
Turn observations into needs and insights, and identify the user's real pain points, expectations, and opportunities.

## Companion Tools

| Tool | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Diary Study | Record repetitive behavior over several days or weeks |
| Focus Group (F2F) | Focus the contextual findings into specific scenarios |
| Online Surveys / Phone Interviews | Expand the sample size beyond the field study |
| User Interviews and Conversations | Complementary use: interviews reveal deeper motivation |
