# Six Thinking Hats

Six Thinking Hats is Edward de Bono's framework for parallel thinking. Instead of arguing from different angles at once, a team uses one thinking mode at a time so discussion stays focused and productive.

## Why Meetings Turn Into Debates

People often bring mixed modes into the same discussion: one person attacks, another defends, another proposes, and the meeting turns into friction instead of collaboration.

Six Thinking Hats solves that by separating the modes. Everyone wears the same hat at the same time, so the team moves in one direction together.

## The Six Hats

| Hat | Mode | Focus | Typical Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| White | Facts | Data, evidence, and missing information | What do we know, and what do we not know? |
| Red | Feelings | Intuition and emotion | What is my gut reaction? |
| Black | Risk | Critique and caution | What could go wrong? |
| Yellow | Value | Benefits and upside | What is the upside and why does it matter? |
| Green | Creativity | New ideas and alternatives | What else could we try? |
| Blue | Process | Facilitation and sequence | What are we doing next? |

The rule is simple: use one hat at a time.

## When to Use It

Six Thinking Hats is especially useful when:

- A discussion has become polarized or chaotic
- A team needs a shared thinking process
- You want a meeting to be more efficient
- You need a structured way to evaluate a complex decision
- Different departments need a common language
- You want to stimulate creativity without losing practical judgment

## Challenger Case Study

The 1986 Challenger disaster is a reminder of what happens when a team does not separate thinking modes clearly.

- The shuttle broke apart 73 seconds after launch
- Seven astronauts died
- The root cause was the failure of an O-ring seal

The larger problem was decision-making. Engineers had risk warnings, but management did not create a structure where black-hat concerns could dominate the conversation before launch.

### What the hats would have added

- White hat: the engineering data and temperature warnings
- Red hat: the unease people felt about the launch
- Black hat: a clear discussion of risk
- Yellow hat: the reasons the launch felt politically attractive
- Blue hat: a facilitator to keep the discussion disciplined

### Key lessons

- Make sure every viewpoint is heard, especially the uncomfortable ones
- Share information across teams instead of isolating it
- Build a process that lets technical concerns surface early

## Five Keys to Effectiveness

1. The blue hat facilitator matters more than people expect.
2. Do not let red-hat feelings and black-hat critique turn into pointless internal conflict.
3. Red hat is input, not final judgment.
4. Use a deliberate sequence instead of letting everyone freewheel.
5. End with a synthesis and a clear next step.

## Edward de Bono

Edward de Bono was a Maltese neurologist, psychologist, and writer. He published *The New Think* in 1967, introduced the idea of lateral thinking, and then developed Six Thinking Hats as a practical thinking system. His work was translated into more than 40 languages and sold more than 25 million copies.

| Year | Milestone |
|---|---|
| 1933 | Born in Valletta, Malta |
| 1967 | Published *The New Think* and popularized lateral thinking |
| 1980s | Major companies began using his training in management programs |
| 2000s | The framework spread in China and other markets |
| 2023 | De Bono died at the age of 91 |
