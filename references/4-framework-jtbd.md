# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

Customers are not buying a product; they are hiring it to do a job. Find the job, and you find the real competitor.

**Authors**: Clayton Christensen, Bob Moesta  
**Book**: *Competing Against Luck* (2016)  
**Publisher**: Mechanical Industry Press  
**Publication Date**: January 2016

## When Should You Use It?

JTBD is best when you need to reconsider who your real competitors are and why users choose you. When user behavior cannot be explained by traditional attributes or preferences, the task lens often reveals unexpected insight.

### Discover Hidden Competitors

You may think your competitors are specific brands, products, or features, but the real competitor is often a workaround or alternative habit.

### Explore Demand Analysis

When you are unsure what users truly need, JTBD helps you redefine the problem. Traditional pain-point research often turns into people talking to themselves.

### Improve Conversion

Understand the user's deeper motivation, identify what blocks the task, and find the leverage point.

### Position the Market

You can only decide where your product or service is better if you understand what it is replacing and why.

### Break Growth Bottlenecks

When acquisition costs keep rising, reframe the problem around the target situation, not the target demographic.

### Improve Marketing Copy

Write in task language so users immediately feel, "This product is for someone like me."

## One Core Job, Five Dimensions

At its core, JTBD identifies the job the user is trying to complete, along with the functional, emotional, and social dimensions that shape the task and the success metrics used to judge it.

### JTBD Framework

```text
┌─────────────┐     ┌─────────────┐
│  Social     │     │  Emotional  │
│  Context    │     │  State      │
└──────┬──────┘     └──────┬──────┘
       │                   │
       ▼                   ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│         The Job to Be Done      │
│                                 │
│    (the task the user wants     │
│     to complete)                │
└─────────────────────────────────┘
       ▲                   ▲
       │                   │
┌──────┴──────┐     ┌──────┴──────┐
│ Functional  │     │  Success    │
│  Context    │     │  Metrics    │
└─────────────┘     └─────────────┘
```

### JTBD Dimensions

| Dimension | Core Question | JTBD View |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| Product Positioning | What functional task is being solved, and in what situation? | What job does the user want to finish? |
| Competitor Definition | What is the real alternative, including "doing nothing"? | What substitutes are users already using? |
| Segmentation | Demographics such as age, gender, or location | The triggering situation: when and why does the job appear? |
| Marketing | Feature-led promotion based on category comparisons | Messaging centered on the user's task and emotional resonance |

## From Harvard Research to a Silicon Valley Standard

JTBD was born in Clayton Christensen's Harvard research, refined through Bob Moesta's practice, and brought to the global product world through *Competing Against Luck*.

### Timeline

**1980s**
**Christensen's Harvard research begins**
While studying disruptive innovation across industries, Christensen realized that the way users "hire" products could not be explained by traditional attributes or preferences.

**2000s**
**Theories of disruption mature**
Christensen and Michael Raynor expanded the ideas in *The Innovator's Dilemma* and introduced an early version of Jobs-to-be-Done.

**2010s**
**Bob Moesta operationalizes the method**
Working with Christensen, Bob Moesta studied startup cases and JTBD interview patterns, helping systematize the framework through research with Uber, Airbnb, Dropbox, and others.

**2016**
**Publication of *Competing Against Luck***
Christensen and colleagues formally articulated JTBD as a product methodology for understanding user demand and product management.

**2020s**
**Becomes a mainstream product framework**
JTBD is now widely used at Amazon, Salesforce, Airbnb, and other large companies, and remains a standard framework for product teams and user research.

## How Intercom Redefined the Boundaries of Customer Service Software

Intercom was initially positioned as live chat software, but growth stalled. Co-founder Des Traynor used JTBD to re-examine the real job users were hiring Intercom to do, and discovered a disruptive insight: the real competitor was not Zendesk, but the Python scripts users had already grown used to.

## Five Steps to Identify the Real Job

The core value of JTBD research is to understand the user's target state. Start by looking at what happened when the user most recently began or stopped using a product.

### Five-Step Method

**01 Identify when the user hires the product**
- Observe the situation in which the user hires the product
- Find the root trigger behind the behavior

**02 Discover the blockers**
- What obstacles did the user encounter while trying to complete the job?
- What pain and anxiety appeared along the way?

**03 Understand the desired outcome**
- What target state does the user want to reach?
- What is success, and how should it be measured?

**04 Find the alternatives**
- Under what conditions does the user look for substitutes?
- Include DIY, Excel, manual processes, or other options

**05 Define the successful scenario**
- What does true task completion look like to the user?
- What is the success criterion, and how can it be measured?

### Step 1 Checklist

```text
✓ Observe when the user hires the product
  → Find the root trigger behind the behavior, whether it is a feature need or a deeper motive
  → Separate functional, emotional, and social jobs; do not look only at the feature layer
  → Redefine positioning and competition using task language
  → Use scenario-based interviews aligned with the JTBD task type
```

### Step 2 Checklist

```text
✓ Discover the blockers
  → What is the actual obstacle in the JTBD process: a functional limit or a cognitive/emotional issue?
  → Define success criteria with the JTBD framework and measure task completion accordingly
  → Use scenario-based interviews to understand the desired job
```
