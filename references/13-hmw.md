# HMW Framework

"How Might We..." turns research findings into design opportunities and opens up creative thinking. The art is to define the solution space without closing it too tightly.

**Posted by**: Designlab | 2023 | Design thinking tool | Reading time: 8 minutes

## User Research Is Done. What Now?

After user research, teams often hit a stall: they have a pile of data, but no clear way to turn it into action.

That is when the "we need wireframing" trap is most dangerous. Do not jump straight from a problem statement to a solution. Pause, and reframe the problem first.

HMW is the bridge for that. It should neither be so narrow that it locks the scope nor so broad that it becomes impossible to act on. It opens a path from one problem to many possible solutions.

## The Art of Balanced Questions

A good HMW sits between too narrow and too broad. Too broad and the team gets lost in ideas; too narrow and creativity gets boxed in.

Spectrum:

Too narrow <-> Too broad
"How might we help users finish an order faster?" <-> "How might we make the world a better place?"

### Examples

1. "How might we help users finish an order faster?"
- Too specific; it limits the solution space.

2. "How might we make shopping more fun?"
- Broad enough to allow design space while still giving direction.

3. "How might we make the world better?"
- Too broad to act on.

### Comment examples

- "This framework helps students define problems more clearly."
- "How might we help users finish an order faster?" is a very good example.
- "How might we make the world better?" is too broad to solve.

### Dimensions

| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Problem | Focus on a specific product feature or user experience |
| Scenario | Target one situation, context, or pain point |
| User group | Aim at a clear audience such as students or older adults |
| Technology | Enter the problem from a technical angle |
| Business / Value | Use current resources as the entry point and lower exploration cost |

## When Should You Use It?

HMW is the bridge from research to design. It helps teams understand problems and generate opportunities.

### Six use cases

1. Brainstorming after user research
2. Starting point for team ideation meetings
3. Preparing for a Design Sprint
4. Breaking a large problem into manageable HMW questions
5. Creative workshops and hackathons
6. Product strategy definition

## IDEO x Bank of America Case Study (2009)

Bank of America hired IDEO to redesign savings for young users. Research found a key insight: people often had a few dollars left in their pocket and never got around to saving it.

IDEO turned that into an HMW question:

"How might we help people save money naturally and without thinking about it?"

### Keep the Change

The idea generated by that HMW became the Keep the Change product:

1. Automatic round-up
- Every purchase is rounded up to the nearest dollar and the difference goes into savings.

2. Immediate feedback
- Users see their savings grow right away, which creates a small emotional reward.

3. Unconscious saving
- The system handles the action automatically, lowering the barrier to entry.

4. Emotional connection
- Saving becomes tied to everyday spending, which increases attachment to the account.

### Results

2.5 million users | $100 million | 1

Active users in the first 3 months | dollars saved | average monthly savings per user

### Why it worked

- It focused on "without thinking about it" instead of "actively saving"
- It left the solution open enough for the team to explore
- It used a natural, emotional entry point

### Key point

- The frame changed from "How do we help people save?" to "How do we make saving happen naturally?"
- That reframing helped inspire later micro-savings products such as Acorns

## Timeline

### From a simple question card to IDEO's creativity engine

The HMW framework grew out of IDEO's design thinking practice in the late 20th century and eventually became a signature element of the discipline.

**1970s**
- Early work by Tim Brown at Stanford on the "How might we..." framing

**1980s**
- IDEO workshops start using HMW questions as a creative tool

**2000s**
- Stanford d.school incorporates HMW into design thinking teaching

**2012**
- Google Ventures and IDEO collaborate on Design Sprint and make HMW a core tool

**2015+**
- HMW becomes a standard part of design, education, government, and social innovation work
