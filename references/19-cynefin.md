# Cynefin Framework

Cynefin is a Welsh word meaning "habitat" or "place of belonging." The framework divides problems into five domains, each with its own decision rule.

The key insight: many product decisions live in the complex domain, but teams often treat them as if they were merely complicated.

## Introduction

One common reason product decisions fail is that leaders simplify too much, overanalyze, or just get lucky. The problem is not the method itself; it is that decision-makers do not understand the nature of the problem.

Cynefin says some problems are simple and some are complex. Different kinds of problems need different responses.

## The Five Domains

### Clear
- Sense -> categorize -> respond
- Cause and effect are obvious
- There is a right answer
- Product examples: refunds, sign-up flow, password recovery

### Complicated
- Sense -> analyze -> respond
- Cause and effect exist but require expertise
- There may be multiple correct answers
- Product examples: choosing a tech stack, designing A/B tests, upgrading database architecture

### Complex
- Probe -> sense -> respond
- Cause and effect can only be understood after the fact
- There is no single right answer
- Product examples: growth strategy, product-market fit for a new feature, pricing strategy

### Chaotic
- Act -> sense -> respond
- No predictable cause and effect
- Immediate action is required
- Product examples: a severe launch bug, a sudden competitor price drop

### Disorder
- You do not know which domain you are in
- A common mistake is to assume something is simple when it is actually complex

## When Should You Use It?

Cynefin is best used before deciding what to do. Ask: what kind of problem is this?

### Use cases

1. Early strategy planning
2. Product prioritization
3. Crisis response
4. Team communication
5. Innovation and experiment design
6. Cross-functional decision making

## COVID-19 Response and Taiwan CDC

The pandemic became a real-world example of all four domains appearing at once.

### Clear
- Mask rationing, health code registration, vaccine booking

### Complicated
- Resource allocation and testing workflow optimization

### Complex
- Transmission prediction and social distancing policy effectiveness

### Chaotic
- Cluster outbreaks and supply chain disruptions

### The CDC's response

- Used clear-domain logic for mask allocation
- Treated booking apps and similar tools as small experiments
- Adjusted policy based on feedback
- Allowed limited controlled failure

## Five Keys

1. Do not over-simplify complex problems
2. Do not treat chaotic problems as if they were complicated
3. Reclassify your problem domain regularly
4. Do not look for a perfect solution first
5. Distinguish simple from complicated clearly

## Timeline

Cynefin was created by Dave Snowden in 1999 while he was leading knowledge management work at IBM. It later became a widely used leadership and change framework.

### Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1999 | Snowden develops Cynefin at IBM |
| 2003 | He founds Cognitive Edge |
| 2007 | A Harvard Business Review article brings Cynefin into mainstream management |
| 2013 | The framework starts being used in large product decision processes |
| 2020 | It is used widely in COVID-19 leadership decisions |
