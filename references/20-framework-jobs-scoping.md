# Jobs Scoping

Jobs Scoping helps define what job a product should be responsible for. It narrows scope, prevents scope creep, and forces practical thinking.

**Author**: Anthony John Stronge  
**Date**: March 28, 2016  
**Read time**: 5 minutes  

## Introduction

Products often keep growing until they become blurry. One common pattern is scope creep: every quarter, more features get added, and the core value gets buried under "nice-to-have" extras.

Jobs Scoping starts by asking: what jobs is this product actually responsible for?

A good scoping decision is not just "we will do X." It is also "we will not do X, because Y is the real user need and X belongs to a different job."

## Job Map: The eight universal stages of a job

Rob Moesta and Chris Spock observed this pattern across thousands of jobs. It is a common language for teams to reason about task structure and align through storytelling.

| Stage | Title | Description | Example |
|------|-------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | Define | Recognize the problem, need, or goal | The user realizes there is something to solve |
| 2 | Search | Research options and compare solutions | The user starts looking for possible solutions |
| 3 | Select | Evaluate the options and choose one | The user selects a specific solution |
| 4 | Acquire | Buy, download, or register | The user gets the solution |
| 5 | Use | Learn and execute the task | The user uses the solution to get the job done |
| 6 | Supplement | Look for extra support or resources | The user needs additional help or tools |
| 7 | Monitor | Track progress and evaluate results | The user checks whether the goal is being met |
| 8 | Complete | Confirm the job is done | The user finishes and reflects on the result |

## When Should You Use It?

Use Jobs Scoping whenever the team needs to define "who we are and who we are not."

### Good fits

- Product positioning is unclear
- Scope creep is getting worse
- Cross-functional teams disagree on the product definition
- Resources are limited and focus matters

## Intercom Case Study

In 2016, Rob Moesta and the Intercom team used a Jobs Scoping exercise to answer a strategic question: are we a customer messaging tool or a CRM?

The answer was neither. They reframed the product around a sharper job.

### Key insight

Users hire Intercom to respond to customers in key moments. That is a "message-driven customer relationship" job.

### Why it matters

That key moment is the core of the job map. If the product is just a pile of buttons and dialogs, and it ignores that moment, users will not buy it.

## How to Run Jobs Scoping

### Step 1: Choose the target job

Choose one concrete job: not "what do users want," but "what are they trying to do in which situation?"

Example: "Reach customer support quickly in an emergency" is a job. "Provide a better customer service experience" is not.

### Step 2: Draw the job map

Use the eight stages and ask:
- What does the user do here?
- What tools or resources do they need?
- What blocks them?

### Step 3: Mark product coverage

Mark which stages the product supports and which stages it does not.

### Step 4: Decide the boundary

For every weakly covered or uncovered stage, ask:
- Is this part of the core job?
- Or is it a different job?

### Step 5: Align and validate

Use the job map to align product, sales, support, and other stakeholders on what the product will and will not do.
