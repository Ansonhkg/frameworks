# Jobs Portfolio

Most products do not solve one job. They solve a portfolio of jobs: core jobs, related jobs, and emotional jobs. The growth opportunity is often hidden in the job that is not yet being served well.

## Why It Matters

When a product focuses only on one obvious job, it can miss the rest of the user's workflow and motivation. A stronger product balances different jobs instead of over-optimizing a single feature.

## Three Types of Work

| Type | What It Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Core Job | The main reason the user comes to the product | WeChat messaging, Taobao search |
| Related Jobs | Supporting tasks around the core job | Emoji, cart, playlists, sync |
| Emotional Jobs | Jobs tied to surprise, status, trust, or delight | "Nudge" features, personalized recommendations |

## When to Use It

- When product growth slows down
- When feature bloat starts to hurt clarity
- When you need to find a new growth engine
- When the product needs a sharper positioning story

## Spotify Case Study

Spotify started as a music listening product, but it discovered that users also wanted discovery, sharing, and audio experiences beyond songs.

By treating podcasts as an underserved emotional job, Spotify expanded from a music player into a broader audio platform.

## How to Review a Jobs Portfolio

1. List the jobs your product already supports.
2. Split them into core, related, and emotional jobs.
3. Score how well each job is being served.
4. Find weak or underserved jobs.
5. Decide whether to improve, add, or drop jobs based on strategy.
