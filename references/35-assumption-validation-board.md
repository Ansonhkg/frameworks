# Assumption Validation Board

The Assumption Validation Board helps teams make business ideas concrete by turning assumptions into testable questions. It is designed to reduce the risk of building the wrong thing.

## Why It Matters

Most products do not fail because the team built the wrong feature set. They fail because the team validated the wrong assumption.

Every product idea rests on assumptions:

- Users have the problem you think they have
- They care enough to change behavior
- They are willing to pay
- Your solution can actually solve the problem

The board helps you test those assumptions early, at low cost, before you commit to a full build.

## Core Formula

The point is to turn a belief into a measurable experiment:

> How might we validate that, if we pursue `[goal]` and observe `[measurable result]`, we will know whether the idea is viable?

## Question Types

| Question | Value Hypothesis | Growth Hypothesis |
|---|---|---|
| What are we validating? | Test whether users are willing to pay for the product | Test whether the marketing channel can bring enough traffic |
| Why are we doing this? | If users will not pay, the pricing or value proposition needs to change | If traffic is too low, we need another channel |
| How will we do it? | Create a simple landing page with a purchase button | Run ads that send users to the landing page |
| When will we do it? | Start today and run for one week | Start today and run for two weeks |
| Who will do it? | Product manager and designer | Marketing manager |
| What counts as success? | 10% of visitors click the purchase button | Cost per click stays below $2 |

## When to Use It

Use the board when you need to validate a new idea quickly:

- New product concepts
- Internal innovation in larger companies
- MVP experiments
- Startup fundraising preparation
- Data-driven growth experiments
- Everyday entrepreneurial thinking

## Zappos

In 1999, Nick Swinmurn wanted to sell shoes online. Instead of building a full store first, he created a simple website with shoe photos and a buy button.

When someone placed an order, he bought the shoes from a local store and shipped them manually. The experiment answered the real question: do people want to buy shoes online?

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Validation cost | $0 plus a few pairs of shoes |
| Outcome | Zappos was later acquired by Amazon for $1.2B |

## Airbnb

Airbnb used the same approach to test whether people would rent rooms from strangers online.

The founders built a simple site, listed rooms, and handled early bookings manually. The experiment proved that the underlying demand was real.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Validation cost | $0 plus a few stays in the rooms |
| Outcome | Airbnb later grew into a company valued at tens of billions |

## Common Mistakes

1. Being vague instead of specific.
2. Confusing correlation with causation.
3. Ignoring sample bias and survivor bias.
4. Using instinct instead of data.
5. Treating assumptions as KPIs.
6. Designing experiments only to confirm the idea, not to falsify it.

## Lean Origins

The board comes from the lean startup tradition, where learning is more valuable than guessing.

| Year | Milestone |
|---|---|
| 2003 | Steve Blank formalizes Customer Development |
| 2011 | Eric Ries publishes *The Lean Startup* and popularizes Build-Measure-Learn |
| 2012 | Ash Maurya adapts lean thinking into the Lean Canvas |
| 2015 | Rob Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test* reinforces the need to validate real problems |
| 2019 | Lean startup methods are combined with design sprint practices in many teams |
