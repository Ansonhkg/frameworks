# Systems Thinking

Systems thinking looks at relationships, not just individual problems. It helps you see why fixing one thing often creates a new problem somewhere else.

## Why Fixes Create New Problems

When teams use a simple problem-solution mindset on a complex system, the result is often a short-term fix and a longer-term mess.

Peter Senge's point is that many problems are not isolated points. They are structural.

## Feedback Loops

Systems thinking commonly uses causal loop diagrams. Two basic loops matter:

- Reinforcing loops make change grow stronger
- Balancing loops push the system back toward equilibrium

## When to Use It

Use systems thinking when:

- A problem keeps coming back
- The cause is in the relationships, not one isolated part
- Linear thinking cannot explain the situation
- A local fix keeps creating global side effects

## Twitter/X Example

Social platforms often show the tension between growth loops and balancing loops.

The lesson is not that "more resources" always help. Sometimes the real leverage point is outside the immediate problem.

## Common Misconceptions

1. A problem is not the same as a symptom.
2. A system is bigger than an organization.
3. A leverage point is not the same as a solution.
4. A feedback loop is not the same as a simple cause-effect chain.
5. Systems thinking is not just problem solving.
6. Systems thinking is not the same as organizational learning.

## History

| Era | Milestone |
|---|---|
| 1940s | Ballistics and control theory lay the foundation |

| 1960s | System thinking spreads into global problem analysis |
| 1980s | Peter Senge's *The Fifth Discipline* brings it mainstream |
| 2000s | Product and tech teams begin applying it more often |
