# Double Diamond

The Double Diamond is a problem-solving process proposed by the British Design Council in 2005. It is widely seen as a classic design-thinking workflow.

## Why do teams keep solving the wrong problem?

Many product failures happen because the team never truly understood the problem. A team may ask, "How do we increase engagement?" or "How do we improve conversion?" and then start adding features and tweaking the UI to make people stay longer.

But the real issue may be that "stay longer" was the wrong target in the first place.

The Crossover example shows the same trap. The product was initially treated as a simple recruiting platform, but users said the interview process was too complex and too long. The team tried to redesign the interface, but the real issue was the hiring model itself.

## Two Diamonds, Four Stages

The Double Diamond has two diamonds:

1. Discover the right problem
2. Deliver the right solution

### Flow

Discover -> Define -> Develop -> Deliver

### Four stages

| Stage | Time | Goal | Key activities |
|------|------|------|----------------|
| Discover | Day 1-2 | Gather information broadly and explore the real problem | Interviews, competitor analysis, data analysis, brainstorming, persona creation |
| Define | Day 3-4 | Distill the core problem and pain points | Cluster analysis, journey maps, HMW statements, design goals |
| Develop | Day 5-7 | Explore many solution directions | Brainstorming, rapid prototyping, user feedback, iteration |
| Deliver | Day 8-10 | Refine and implement the chosen solution | Launch, training, data review, marketing, iteration |

## When Should You Use It?

Use the Double Diamond when the problem itself is still unclear. If the problem is already well defined, you can move faster through the solution side.

### Good fits

- Complex problems where the real issue is unknown
- Teams that disagree on the definition of the problem

### When you can skip the second diamond

- Purely technical projects where the problem is already clear
- Situations where the problem has already been defined by another method

## NHS x IDEO Case Study

In 2013, the UK National Health Service and IDEO ran a project to improve emergency room patient experience. The waiting room was noisy, crowded, and full of information asymmetry, which caused anxiety and frustration for families.

### Discover

- The team spent 3-4 weeks in emergency departments in London hospitals and interviewed more than 20 patients and relatives
- Nurses were often too busy to explain what was happening
- The key insight: patients were not only worried about waiting time; they were worried about not knowing what was happening or when they would be seen

### Define

- Reframe "How do we shorten waiting time?" into "How do we help patients feel seen and reassured while they wait?"
- HMW: "How might we make every patient feel that they are being cared for?"

### Develop

- Ideas included live status screens, smart bands for families, and a "patient experience officer"
- The chosen solution was an electronic board in the waiting area that updated patient status in real time

### Deliver

- The system was piloted in two hospitals for three months
- It was later expanded to 10 major hospitals
- Patient satisfaction rose by 25% and complaints dropped by 40%

### Core insight

The first diamond showed that "waiting time" was only the surface. The real pain was uncertainty created by missing information.

## Five Principles

1. The first diamond matters more than the second
2. Define should output HMW questions, not solutions
3. Discover should produce many candidate "fake problems"
4. For complex problems, be willing to use other tools too
5. Do not confuse "simple" with "complicated"
