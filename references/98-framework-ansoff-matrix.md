# Ansoff Growth Matrix

The Ansoff Matrix is a classic growth framework built around two questions: are we using an existing or new market, and are we using an existing or new product?

## Why It Matters

There is no fifth growth direction. The matrix forces teams to be explicit about where growth should come from and what risk level they are accepting.

## The Four Growth Paths

| Market / Product | Existing Product | New Product |
|---|---|---|
| Existing Market | Market Penetration | Product Development |
| New Market | Market Development | Diversification |

| Path | Meaning | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Market Penetration | Sell more of the current product in the current market | Low |
| Product Development | Build new products for current customers | Medium |
| Market Development | Take current products to new markets | Medium |
| Diversification | Enter new products and new markets | High |

## When to Use It

- Annual or quarterly strategy planning
- When growth slows
- When deciding where to put limited resources
- When evaluating a new product idea
- During acquisition or investment review

## Amazon Case Study

Amazon is a strong example of multi-path growth:

- Market penetration: Prime, recommendations, and pricing optimization
- Product development: Kindle, Alexa, and AWS
- Market development: international expansion and enterprise reach
- Diversification: Amazon Studios, pharmacy, Ring, and Zoox

## How to Evaluate Growth Options

1. Define your current position.
2. Score the opportunity and risk in each quadrant.
3. Decide which path deserves the most resources.
4. Write a concrete action plan.
5. Review the matrix regularly as the market changes.

## Limitations

| Limitation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Oversimplifies reality | Markets and products are not purely binary |
| Ignores execution difficulty | A strategy can be attractive but still fail in practice |
| Static view | The meaning of "new" changes over time |
| Misses synergy | Growth paths often reinforce each other |

## History

| Period | Development |
|---|---|
| 1957 | Igor Ansoff publishes the original growth logic |
| 1960s | The framework is expanded in *Corporate Strategy* |
| 1980s | It becomes a core MBA and consulting tool |
| 2000s | Internet companies reframe the quadrants |
| 2017+ | Strategy discussions continue to use it as a baseline model |
